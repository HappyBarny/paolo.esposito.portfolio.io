About this project

This project starts from a 2 weeks University challenge that I’ve started with my classmates: Alessio Viola, Elisa della Chiesa and Sara.

With them I’ve made all the research, analysis, developed storyboards and a scenario where we started to think about a possible solution.
The Informational Architecture and the final prototype was developed entirely by me.

The challenge

Local businesses are increasingly under pressure from the vast reach and convenience of large-scale online distributors, making it difficult for them to compete.
In a world where customers are drawn to the simplicity of online shopping, protecting and revitalizing local businesses has become a crucial challenge.

While these businesses offer unique products and services that resonate with their communities, they struggle to match the digital presence and efficiency of larger competitors.
The challenge lies in creating a system that not only shields these local businesses from the dominance of online giants but also helps them relaunch and thrive in a competitive digital landscape.

The Research

For this project we decided to do a research that aimed to understand how families in the neighborhoods do their grocery shopping.
Investigating and researching the needs of local families is here more than essential because they form the backbone of Italian neighborhoods.

In this context we decided to combine narrative interviews with business ethnography to obtain a comprehensive view of their habits, needs, and challenges.
We combined Narrative Interviews with Business Ethnography researches for several reasons:

  1. Insights validation: Interviews provided us with a series of qualitative data that were based on self-reported experiences. Sometimes people’s perceptions of their habits might differ from their actual behavior.
    Shadowing them during their grocery shopping leads us to validate or challenge the information they shared with us during the interview.

  2. Contextual understanding: Ethnography adds context to the interview data. With Business Ethnography research we could make several observations that could help us explain why people make certain choices that might not come up during the interview.

  3. Catching Unconscious Behaviors: People often have certain habits or behaviors that they are not fully aware of.
    Making groceries with them allowed us to catch that series of small but significant details that they always do. Through simple observation we captured a series of unconscious behaviors that were crucial for us while we were designing our final product.

The Insights

Using the data gathered from our research we created different personas to represent key user groups.





These two personas became a reference point throughout the design process.
To have a more clear vision of the problem we also decided to identify a common user journey map pointing out opportunities and pain points that we have learned directly on the field.




Our findings clearly show that many people don’t always know their neighborhood as well as they’d like, and they’re constantly on the lookout for fresh, high-quality products.
They also want more transparency about the items they buy, especially knowing where they come from.
In our research, we discovered that people want to know ahead of time where they can find specific products locally, and they’re looking for ways to make their grocery shopping faster and more efficient.

This growing demand for a personalized and convenient shopping experience reveals a huge opportunity for an app that connects people with local businesses.
By providing better product information, making it easier to discover fresh, locally sourced products, and simplifying the shopping process, the app can help local stores meet these needs while building stronger relationships within the neighborhood.

To sum it up, people:

  • Aren’t always familiar with their local area
  • Want to spend more time on things they enjoy, and less time on grocery shopping
  • Prefer fresh, quality food
  • Need an easier way to find the products they’re looking for in nearby stores.

Our research didn’t end there. To gain a more comprehensive understanding, we extended our study by conducting a contextual inquiry research with local sellers and small businesses in our neighborhoods.
This allowed us to explore the relationship between these businesses and their community in greater depth.

We discovered that many local events and special offers often go unnoticed by residents as these businesses struggle to achieve visibility in the neighborhood. Despite offering unique products and services, many people remain unaware of what these local shops can provide.

This lack of visibility and awareness hinders their ability to compete effectively with larger retailers.
This gap in awareness and communication presents a clear opportunity to improve the connection between local businesses and the community, aligning their offerings more closely with the needs and preferences of neighborhood families.

How might we... ?

Having all this information in my hands, I started to wonder how I could act on the insights that emerged and how to deal with the various pain points that we found during our research
So we asked ourself, how might we...

  • Help people discover hidden gems and local stores in their neighborhood more easily?

  • Streamline grocery shopping so that people can spend less time on it and more time on what they love?

  • Provide real-time information about where to find specific products locally, reducing the guesswork in grocery shopping?

  • How might we make it easier for local businesses to showcase their high-quality products building trust with customers?

  • How might we help local businesses showcase their events and engage the community in real time?

This approach framed all the key problems and opened up opportunities for creative solutions!

The idea

Having scouted the main pain points of the customer journey, the areas of opportunity while thinking about the challenge and all the insights that we gathered during our research, the solution came up naturally.
We created “Quartiere Vivo,” an app designed to:

  • Help people explore and discover more of their neighborhood

  • Make it easy to create grocery lists and choose whether to have items delivered or pick them up in-store

  • Give local businesses a way to highlight their events, deals, and special offers

  • Help people find out what services local shops provide

  • Make it simple for people to locate the exact products they need right in their own neighborhood

Developing the prototype

The journey for every user begins with an interactive map, designed to help them explore and connect with local businesses in their neighborhood. This map offers a simple, intuitive experience where users can easily zoom in and out or use filters to discover shops, events, and services they may not have known were just around the corner.
The focus of this phase is on discovery, making it easy for users to uncover what's nearby based on their location, while fostering a deeper connection with their local community.


Users can quickly find local shops that offer the specific products they’re looking for, all within their neighborhood. The app streamlines the search process, making it easy to locate items without the need for extensive browsing.

Additionally, sponsored products and deals are prominently featured at the top of search results, giving users instant access to special offers and promotions from nearby businesses, helping them discover great deals while supporting local shops.
